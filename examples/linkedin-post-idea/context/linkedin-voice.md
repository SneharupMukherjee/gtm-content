# Voice: LinkedIn Content — Jane Doe, Acme

## Purpose

This voice file governs LinkedIn hook writing. It adapts Jane Doe's email voice for public
content. The audience is the same — coaches drowning in admin — but the context is different:
they're scrolling a feed, not reading a message addressed to them.

---

## Tone

Warm, direct, slightly frustrated on their behalf. Like a peer coach who gets it, not a brand
that's trying to sell something. Assertive enough to stop the scroll. Calm enough to feel
credible. Never preachy. Never corporate.

---

## ICP Pain Language — Use These Verbatim or Very Close

These come from real community posts. The closer to verbatim, the more the reader feels seen.

- "part-time admin, not a full-time coach"
- "overwhelmed by admin work"
- "buried in admin work"
- "discovery turned into pure admin"
- "the hour after every session"
- "losing half my day to admin before the real work starts"
- "too many tools that don't talk to each other"
- "the most time-consuming admin task"
- "feel like I'm running a spreadsheet, not a coaching practice"
- "admin ceiling" (limiting growth)

**ICP context (internalize this):**
The coaching audience earns $3K–$10K/month with 8–15 active clients. They spend 7–10 hours
a week on post-call admin: writing notes, drafting follow-ups, manually scheduling next calls,
tracking payments across Calendly + Stripe + Zoom + Notion + Gmail. They got into coaching to
help people, not to manage tools. They feel like part-time admins. They're slightly skeptical
of "all-in-one" promises because they've been burned before. They value time over money when
it comes to solutions.

---

## Banned Words

Never use these in any hook or output content:

leverage, optimize, utilize, ecosystem, robust, cutting-edge, streamline, synergy,
solutions, empower, actionable, impactful, transformative, seamless, frictionless,
"In today's world", "As you may know", "The truth is", "Real talk", "Let that sink in",
"thought leadership", "authentic", "journey", "space" (as in "in this space"),
"pivot", "disrupt", "game-changer", "unpack", "double-click on", "circle back",
"move the needle", "best practices", "at the end of the day"

---

## Formatting Rules

- No em dashes — use short sentences instead
- No semicolons
- Always use contractions: don't, you're, it's, here's, I've, they're, we're
- Max 1 exclamation point per output file (not per hook — per the entire generated file)
- No engagement bait: never "Comment YES", "Tag a coach", "Drop a [emoji] if...", "Repost if..."
- No product name ("Acme") in hooks — hooks are content, not ads
- Specific over vague: "7 hours a week" beats "hours", "Calendly + Stripe + Zoom" beats "multiple tools"
- Short sentences. The first 5 words carry the most weight.

---

## Hook Rhythm

The first line must work alone. If someone only reads the first sentence, they should still
feel something. Short, declarative, concrete.

**Good first lines:**
- "Most coaches think this is normal."
- "If you're still doing this manually..."
- "I've noticed something across hundreds of coaches."
- "You didn't become a coach to do this."
- "The session ends. Then the real work starts."
- "Seven tools to run one coaching practice. None of them talk to each other."

**Bad first lines:**
- "In today's fast-paced coaching landscape..."
- "As coaches, we all know that..."
- "Have you ever thought about why..."
- "Here's something nobody tells you about..."
- "I want to talk about something important."
- "This might be controversial, but..."

---

## Voice Notes Per Hook Type

### Contrarian
Challenge a belief, not a person. The refutation should be calm, not aggressive.
"Most coaches think X. They're wrong." is good. "Stop doing X or you're killing your business."
is not. Calm credibility wins over loud provocation.

The belief being challenged should be something the coach has genuinely internalized — not
something obviously wrong. Make them feel "wait, is that true about me?"

### Identity
Speak to the role conflict, not the failure. The coach isn't stupid for doing things manually —
they followed the standard advice. Acknowledge the trap before naming it.

"You became a coach to [real aspiration]. Not to [specific admin pain]." is warm, not
accusatory. Never imply they've been doing it wrong. Imply the situation is wrong.

Use specific admin pain from the ICP list. "Not to spend the hour after every session writing
notes" beats "Not to do admin work."

### Insight/Observation
This only works if it sounds like it comes from genuine observation, not a pitch.
"I've noticed..." or "Something I keep seeing..." signals that. The insight must be
specific enough to feel real — not "coaches are overwhelmed" (too generic) but "coaches
with 10+ clients are losing the hour after every session to the same three tasks" (specific).

End with a colon. The hook's job is to earn the click into the rest of the post, not to
deliver the whole insight in one sentence.

---

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any hook set:
1. Read all 9 hooks aloud. Does any one sound like an ad? Revise it.
2. Check for "Acme" — must not appear anywhere. Remove if found.
3. Check for em dashes and semicolons — remove any found.
4. Count exclamation points across all 9 hooks — reduce to max 1 total.
5. Pick the weakest hook. Ask: would a coach recognize themselves in this? If not, rewrite.
